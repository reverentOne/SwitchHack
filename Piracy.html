Thoughts on Piracy

MOST COMMON ARGUMENT FOR PIRACY

"The developers don't lose money because of me as I would have never paid for them"

While being a fantastic argument, it must be considered in proper context. The individual decision-making that goes into whether a person would have purchased a game is rarely made in isolation because the existence of piracy as an alternative itself influences that very decision-making process.

In 99% of piracy cases copywrite infringement is not enforced. This may differ depending on your country, but in the United States nobody has EVER been arrested or charged exclusively for downloading pirated games. The 1% punished are those distributing pirated games. The reason being that prosecuting criminal copyright infringement requires that the infringer acted "for the purpose of commercial advantage or private financial gain."

Utilitarian argument for piracy

According to Utilitarianism, to justify the morality of Piracy you merely need show that your stealing produces a net positive for the world. This can be difficult to do conclusively. There is no direct harm done through piracy, so one must weigh potential harm vs. potential benefits. Potential harm incudes loss of game sales. Depending on the game this can have a large or small impact on future game development and employment of those in the gaming industry. Potential benefits include personal enjoyment from playing the pirated game that is otherwise unobtanable, unspent money that can go towards any number of beneficial causes, Free advertisement of the game if you talk about it with your social network, etc.

Kantian argument for piracy

One of the central philosophical concepts according to Immanuel kant is the Categorical imperative: Act only according to that maxim whereby you can, at the same time, will that it should become a universal law.

The maxim that "you should always pirate games" would lead to no more development of, or a complete buisness restructure of, games if it became a universal law. One must come up with another maxim to meet the demands of the categorical imperative. One such maxim might be "you should pirate games only if you are unable to reasonably afford it, would not have purchased it anyways, or the game has already sold a significant number of copies." If this maxim became univeral law, nobody would be harmed, game develpment would continue as usual, and many individuals would benefit from the game who would otherwise not be able to.

Impact of Piracy on Game Sales

A study that surveyed 30,000 consumers in six EU countries (UK, Germany, France, Spain, Poland, and Sweden) and cost nearly half a million dollars found that unlike other forms of piracy, pirating video games actually increased game sales!
